# Pinnochio

This skill produces copy that passes two tests simultaneously:

1. No human editor, AI detector, or trained reader flags it as machine-generated.
2. The copy performs: it holds attention, moves the reader toward action, and feels like a specific person wrote it for a specific audience.

These goals reinforce each other. The patterns that trigger AI detection are the same patterns that make copy generic, flat, and forgettable. Fixing one fixes both.

## The Root Problem

AI copy fails because of statistical averaging. Language models default to the most common phrasing, the safest structure, and the blandest tone. The result reads like a composite of every mediocre blog post on the internet. Readers sense it immediately, even if they can't name why.

Good copy does the opposite. It has texture, rhythm variation, opinion, and specificity. It sounds like someone sat down and thought about this one thing for this one audience.

## Voice Construction

Every piece of copy needs a voice before it needs words. Before writing, establish:

**Who is speaking?** A founder? A brand account? A sales rep? A product team? Each has a different register. A founder writes with personal stakes. A brand account writes with collective identity. A sales rep writes with direct address. Match the voice to the speaker.

**Who is reading?** A CISO evaluating vendors processes information differently than a small business owner Googling "do I need cybersecurity." Write for the reader's vocabulary, pain points, and decision-making frame.

**What does the reader feel right now?** Before your copy, the reader exists in some emotional and informational state. Your opening sentence meets them there. If they're frustrated, acknowledge frustration. If they're curious, reward curiosity. If they're skeptical, lead with proof, not claims.

If the user doesn't specify these parameters, ask. Copy without a defined voice and audience is copy that sounds like nobody wrote it for nobody.

## Sentence-Level Rules

These rules produce copy that reads human and performs well. They are non-negotiable.

### Vary sentence length deliberately

AI writes sentences of roughly equal length. Humans don't. Mix short punches with longer explanatory sentences. A three-word sentence after a complex one creates emphasis. Read the draft out loud. If every sentence takes the same number of breaths, rewrite.

**Flat (AI-pattern):**
"Our platform provides comprehensive visibility into your network assets. It enables security teams to identify vulnerabilities quickly and efficiently. The intuitive dashboard makes it easy to prioritize remediation efforts."

**Alive:**
"You get full visibility into every asset on your network. Where are the gaps? The dashboard shows you, ranked by severity, so your team fixes what matters first."

### Lead with the concrete, not the abstract

AI opens with category statements ("In today's environment..."). Humans open with specifics. Start with a number, a name, a scenario, or a direct statement. Never start with a throat-clearing generality.

**Dead opening:** "Organizations face increasing cybersecurity challenges in an evolving threat environment."

**Live opening:** "Last quarter, 68% of breaches started with a phished credential. One click. That's the gap you're closing."

### Write asymmetric paragraphs

AI paragraphs are roughly the same length. Three to four sentences each, stacked uniformly. Human writers vary paragraph length because ideas vary in size. A one-sentence paragraph is a tool. Use it when something needs to land hard.

### Kill the mirror structure

AI loves parallel construction: "Not only X, but also Y." "From A to B." "Whether you're [persona 1] or [persona 2]." These structures feel manufactured because they are. State things directly instead of balancing them across a fulcrum.

### Use contractions

"We're" not "We are." "You'll" not "You will." "Don't" not "Do not." Formality without contractions signals either legal documents or machines. Copy is a conversation, and conversations use contractions.

### Use sentence fragments (selectively)

Fragments signal a human voice. "Worth it." "Every time." "Not even close." AI rarely produces fragments because they're grammatically incomplete. Drop one in when the rhythm calls for it.

## Structural Patterns

### The opening line does one job: earn the second line

Don't summarize. Don't introduce the topic. Don't provide context. The first sentence exists to create enough curiosity, recognition, or tension that the reader continues. Everything else can wait.

For email subject lines, the same rule applies at a smaller scale. The subject line earns the open. Nothing else.

### One idea per section

AI copy drifts between ideas within paragraphs because the model generates sequentially without a structural plan. Good copy commits to one idea per paragraph or section and transitions cleanly to the next. If you find a paragraph serving two purposes, split it.

### End on action or implication, not summary

AI wraps up with "In summary" or a restatement of everything above. Copy should end on the thing you want the reader to do or feel. A CTA. A provocative question. A final specific detail that lingers. Never recap.

## Channel-Specific Guidance

### Social media (LinkedIn, X/Twitter, Facebook)

- Open with the most surprising or specific thing, not background
- Front-load value in the first two lines (before the "see more" fold)
- One post, one idea. If you have three ideas, write three posts
- Avoid hashtag stuffing. Zero to two maximum, and only if the platform rewards them
- Write how people talk on that platform. LinkedIn tolerates longer form. X demands compression. Facebook sits in between
- Use line breaks aggressively for readability on mobile
- Don't announce what you're about to say ("I want to share 5 lessons I learned..."). Share the first lesson. The reader will keep going if it's good

### Email (sales, marketing, newsletters)

- Subject lines: 4-8 words. Specific. No clickbait that the body can't deliver on
- First sentence: relevant to the reader's world, not yours
- Body: short paragraphs, clear visual hierarchy, one primary CTA
- Close with the CTA, not a pleasantry. "Reply with your availability" beats "I look forward to hearing from you"
- Sales emails: personalize the first sentence to something real about the recipient. A recent post they wrote, a company milestone, a specific challenge in their industry segment. Never fake personalization with vague compliments

### Landing pages and sales pages

- Headline: outcome the reader wants, stated plainly
- Subhead: how you deliver that outcome, in one sentence
- Proof early: testimonial, stat, case study reference, or specific claim within the first scroll
- Features described as outcomes. "Encrypted DNS" becomes "Your DNS queries stay private, even on public wifi"
- CTA buttons: action verb + outcome. "Start my free trial" beats "Submit." "See pricing" beats "Learn more"

### Ad copy (paid social, search, display)

- Character limits force precision. Write the full message first, then cut to fit
- One value proposition per ad. Don't cram
- Search ads: mirror the searcher's intent language. If they searched "affordable SIEM," your headline includes "affordable SIEM," not "cost-effective security analytics platform"
- Display/social ads: pattern interrupt. The reader is scrolling past everything. Your copy needs to say something unexpected enough to stop the thumb

## Word-Level Discipline

### Banned patterns (AI tells in copy)

These phrases and constructions will get flagged by editors, detectors, and attentive readers. Do not use them.

**Openings to avoid:**
"In today's...", "In an era of...", "As [industry] continues to...", "When it comes to...", "Are you looking for...", "Imagine a world where..."

**Filler verbs:**
"leverage," "utilize," "streamline," "optimize," "empower," "enable," "facilitate," "drive" (as in "drive results"), "spearhead," "champion"

**Fake emphasis:**
"truly," "remarkably," "incredibly," "absolutely," "game-changing," "revolutionary," "cutting-edge," "world-class," "best-in-class," "industry-leading," "next-generation," "state-of-the-art"

**Structural tells:**
"Not only... but also...", "Whether you're... or...", "From X to Y," "Here's the thing:", "Let's be honest:", "The truth is:", "Here's why:", "Think about it:"

**Closing tells:**
"In conclusion," "To summarize," "At the end of the day," "The bottom line is," "Don't miss out," "Act now before it's too late"

### Preferred alternatives

Instead of abstract verbs, use specific ones. "Cuts incident response time from 4 hours to 20 minutes" beats "streamlines your incident response." Instead of superlatives, use evidence. "Used by 2,400 security teams" beats "industry-leading platform."

When you need a strong verb, pick one with a physical or visual quality: "strips away," "pins down," "closes the gap," "drops into," "pulls from." These feel authored because they carry sensory weight.

## The Editing Pass

After drafting, run these checks in order:

1. **Read it out loud.** If any sentence feels awkward to say, rewrite it. If you hear a rhythm that sounds like a press release, rewrite it.

2. **Check the opening.** Does line one earn line two? Or does it generalize? Rewrite if it generalizes.

3. **Scan for AI vocabulary.** Check against the banned patterns list above. Replace every hit.

4. **Check paragraph lengths.** Are they all the same? Vary them.

5. **Check sentence lengths.** Same test. Vary them.

6. **Find the opinion.** Good copy has a point of view. If the draft reads like it could come from any company in the industry, it needs sharper positioning.

7. **Count the claims without evidence.** Every claim ("faster," "more secure," "easier") needs a specific proof point, example, or comparison next to it. Strip unsupported claims or add support.

8. **Check the close.** Does it end on action or implication? Or does it summarize? Fix if it summarizes.

## When the User Gives Minimal Direction

Sometimes the user says "write a LinkedIn post about X" with no other context. In that case, ask for:

1. Who is the audience? (Role, seniority, industry)
2. What should the reader do after reading? (Click, reply, think differently, share)
3. Any specific data points, examples, or stories to include?

If the user wants you to proceed without this context, make reasonable assumptions, state them briefly at the top of the output, and write the copy. Don't stall the workflow with excessive questions.
